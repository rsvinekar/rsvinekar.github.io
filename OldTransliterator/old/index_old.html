Romanakßarí

An experiment with Indian languages

Romanákßarí is not a one-to-one transliteration interface. It is designed as an orthography, a proof of concept, and does some postprocessing according to language-specific rules. This allows us to get the same basic indic script output using different inputs. One may type 'e', 'é' or 'ai' to get the same output ए. or one may type 'ia' or 'ya' to get य . Capital or small letters make no difference to final output.

This allows us to add semantic cues to the write-up while preserving phonetic information. This makes writing Sanskrit, or other languages, more informative and intuitive than devanágarí. It helps us add grammatical information that helps disambiguate meaning more than devanágarí does, while still giving the ability to get devanagari output accurately. This provides a great teaching tool. Use the US-International Keyboard (present on ALL operating systems by default. You have to just activate it). This keyboard allows us to type Western European languages, like German, French, Icelandic etc. in addition to English. Romanákßarí makes full use of this already present common keymap.

Some points to keep in mind :

  • Acute accent or letter doubling can be used for writing long vowels
  • use ae and ao for ऐ and औ . ai and au map to ए and ओ . This is deliberately different from existing standards, as it is Grammatically consistent, while still maintaining phonetics. ái and áu should technically map same as ae and ao , but this doesn't currently work properly with sandhi postprocessing on.(trivial to fix though)
  • þ , ð , ñ , ð , ø are retroflexes : they are chosen because they look different. Note ñ is retroflex n, not palatal n.
  • Velar n is ng, palatal n is nÿ . The ÿ is not compulsary and generally deduced from it's environment.
  • Syllabic r and l are not separately marked. If surrounded by consonants, it is syllabic.
  • ' is a dummy consonant or glottal stop. : is a dummy vowel, or avagraha/alif vasla/elided vowel. Use these if r doesn't match your requirement and be syllabic.
  • x is the visarga. X generically stands for a debuccalized, lenited consonant, generally s or r, or in Arabic, t.

Unlike Sanskrit, x may be used as a consonant, where it stands for ह . This ह however is unvoiced, not voiced like Sanskrit h. Useful for Persian words written in Sanskrit. E.g. If one wishes to write Ahura Mazda in Sanskrit, write it as Axura Mazda. The x is a lenited s, from Asura. This reflects both etymology and the fact that the word meaning has changed (Asura has negative meanings, Axura has positive). Similarly m and n may be used as consonants or vowels.

Romanakßarí allows more freedom in spelling. Devanágarí or Brahmi scripts in general have played a role in keeping the phonetic integrity of sanskrit intact. However their rigid phonetic model is both boon and bane. The bane is because it cannot adapt to other languages. It was designed for Prakrits, not even Sanskrit, which were then modified (rather shabbily, if one compares the care Sanskritists paid to other aspects of the language). Prakrits don't have many Samyuktakshars, and have a large percentage of 'a' as vowels, so the brahmi script is extremely well designed for them. For Sanskrit however, it shows flaws.

Suppose one writes the word Indiá . Devanágarí can handle it either as इन्दिआ , इन्द्या or as इन्दिया. All three are wrong : they would be Indi'á , Indyá and Indiyá. The first has a glottal stop, the second is too short, the third is too long. Devanágarí model cannot accomodate this.

Devanágarí can handle only two levels of vowel-consonant heirarchy. This plays a role in people mislead or misunderstanding Sanskrit itself. The word शत्र्वोः is not śatṛvoḥ as a literal transliteration would give, but Shatruox in Romanakßarí. This makes the root Shatru and it's endings -ox easy to understand. The status of r as ṛ or as consonant is dependent on context. In this case, it is NOT a syllabic, but writing it accurately is hampered by the rigid sound model of Devanágarí. There are a Lot of such examples. Writing the correct way makes things clearer, both phonetically and semantically (due to grammatical regularity). Since Devanágarí has a lot of legacy behind it, one must learn it for Sanskrit. But even in India, the literacy of devanágarí is questionable. The Roman script is understood by vast majority of the world population, and hence is the best vehicle for popularising this beautiful language. People have recognized this problem in the past, and attempted to rectify it. This is done in Marathi and Nepali for instance. Daryá दर्या , Dariá दर्‍या , Barxáðx बर्‍हाड़् Ácárya आचार्य , Ácária आचार्‍य Garyo : गर्यो . Gario : गर्‍यो

These above are examples of eye-lash repha used in Marathi and Nepali. Your browser may or may not show them properly (firefox does, chrome doesn't on my system. Using non-standard Private Area letters  from the Sanskrit 2003 or Siddhanta font, Marathi Ácária (the pickle maker) should look like this : आचाय as opposed to Sanskrit Ácárya आचार्य (the great teacher worth emulating).

The eyelash repha is used to denote that r is an onset (consonantal form), inspite of being followed by a consonant and preceeded by a vowel (which usually tells us r should be part of the nucleus of the consonant). The following consonant, usually a semivowel or aspirated vowel, forms part of the nucleus and isn't really a consonant. The syllables then split differently.ह or र्‍ह means an aspirated r, where r is the beginning of the syllable. In most Sanskrit cases, r is actually a vowel, ar, ir, ur, ár etc. arising out of processes like guña. When it is not, this distinction is ignored. Pari + ávaraña = पर्‍यावरण , not पर्यावरण. However this distinction is ignored, and has been long enough for it to reflect within the meter of poetry composed after Sanskrit ceased to be a mothertongue of any living population.

Applications :

  • Teaching.
  • Programming. Easier to break up words into POS tags. Context easier to understand in Sanskrit
  • Include words from languages like Arabic into Sanskrit write-up fairly accurately (as far as phonetics is concerned).
  • Design a new dialect/artificial language based on Sanskrit (as much conservative as possible).
    • This dialect will be geared for modern usages, with the ability and rules to incorporate foreign words (place names etc.) from major languages (Old Persian, Latin, Greek, Arabic, Chinese etc. in that order. Older languages of the IE family are closer to Sanskrit than newer ones).
    • Incorporate modern day scientific nomenclature.
    • Use and encourage smaller words in Sanskrit, rather than the formal Classical forms (aste and sídati instead of upavißati, as an example. The latter is not wrong, but it is a mouthful, and should be used in formal situations. Commands like 'ae' (from á-eti come) instead of 'ágaccha' (á-gacchati come) for the same reasons. The more complex forms should be introduced later, and let the user decide which is better.
    • Disambiguation and fine distinctions should be kept in mind
